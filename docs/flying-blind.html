James Scott argues that the state seeks to control what it can observe. But today’s state is often flying blind, unable to force legibility onto the phenomena for which it has a democratic demand to control.

What is the British state unable to track?

  • how many people are in the country?
  • how many houses were built last year?
  • how many houses are left vacant?
  • How effective are the charities that receive state funding??
  • How much energy do individuals use?
  • How many buildings still fitted with the type of cladding that exacerbated the Grenfell fire?
  • The scale of child sexual exploitation, especially group-based (“grooming gangs”)

There are lots of well-documented problems with the UK's official statistical body, the ONS. As a result, we don't really know how much the economy is growing or what are the inflation figures really are.

International examples?

  • DOGE debates reveal if nothing else that the federal budget is obscure even to people responsible for overseeing it

Why is this important?

  • Resource misallocation, Hayek information problems
  • Democratic legitimacy: decision-makers need to be able to effect the changes for which they are elected
  • Quality of governance: policy should respond to the way the world actually is, not the way the world appears to be at its most legible points
  • Private sector and civil society depend on reliable state data to plan investment
  • Historical precedent: successful statebuilding efforts in the past are those where state capacity enables observability (Domesday book, Napoleon’s cadastre, 19th century Prussian civil service); deep failures have been where observability was limited (Scott egs, Gosplan, mid-century US urban renewal)

What’s going on?

  • These are complex things to measure
  • Legacy IT, fragmented mandates, data silos, devolution, latency
  • British aversion to state control - we find it quite satisfying that the state is distant (cf ID cards)
  • Opacity rents - arbitrage opportunities from informational asymmetries
  • Lack of institutional capacities - gutted by austerity, cf trains you need a good buyer, a lot of the British state lacks good technical capacity, civil service not structured to reward it
    • In some important senses ‘observability’ is the most important measure of state capacity

  • Everythingism
